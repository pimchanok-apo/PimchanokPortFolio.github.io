Urbanisation and Mental Disorder

Understand Mental conditions across the world Using Regression model, generated by Python

Project Overview

Since past decade, the world has faced the urbanisation process which is regarding to the changes in cities’ characteristic and the migration of population. Urbanisation leads to positive outcome of economic growth in both developed and developing countries. Urbanisation has also led to industrialisation, which mainly affects the level of specialisation of labour and the development of several sectors in economy. However, the rapid sociocultural change due to changes in urban neighbourhood composition could lead to individual’s health conditions including mental health disorder, one of serious disease burden which likely to associate with total number of death case among the world population (Chen et al., 2017). Since people’s health conditions affect provision of living condition and likely to associated with economic costs, it is necessary to gain more understanding in people’s mental condition during the rapid urbanisation process in this world. This study aims to examine the association of urbanisation and mental disorders status across the world. Apart from the urbanisation indicator that assumed to have relationship with mental health condition, we would discover the likelihood of economic impact on people’s mental health condition.

Data and Methodology

The majority of data which associated with estimates of mental health and substance use disorders by age and sex prevalence ratio are based on Institute of Health Metrics and Evaluation (IHME), Global Burden of Disease (GBD). Nevertheless, the underreporting of depression disorders means difficulty to define the prevalence of disorders. Therefore, the data presented in this study is based on an estimate of medical and national records, epidemiological data, in addition to surveys and meta-regression modelling due to the scarcity of raw data (Dattani et al., 2021).

To analyse data, I decide to use linear regression method to test the relationship between variables. I define mental disorders prevalent as Y variable, then define total urban population and GDP per capita as X variables. If there is correlation between Y and X at significant level 0.05, P>|t| of each variables should less than 0.05.

Findings

Main finding of this study provides the OLS linear regression which shows the coefficient of constant term, urbanisation rate and GDP per capita and P>|t| of each variable. Provide that P>|t| > alpha 0.05 means the significant of the variable, I found that there is the positive relationship between ratio of total population in urban area and mental disorders prevalence estimation, as we can see from the coefficient 0.0214 and probability P>|t| = 0.011 at significant level 0.05. This coefficient means if the urbanisation rate in 1 country goes up by 1%, prevalence of mental health and substance use disorders will increase 0.0214%. Whereas GDP per capita is not the best variable to interpret mental health disorders ratio. With the coefficient 1.764e-05 and and probability P>|t| = 0.058 which is exceed alpha 0.05, we reject the hypothesis that GDP per capita have a relationship with mental and substance use disorder with 5% significant level.

Conclusion

According to the results of study, I found that the results state the positive relationship between urbanisation rate and mental health situation, demonstrate by the positive coefficient value and significant level at alpha 0.05. As well as the discussion by several research (Gong et al., 2012, Yeh et al., 2011), main findings of this study suggest that rapid urbanisation and living in urban area could lead to harmful urban lifestyle for urban people. However, even if Chen et al. (2014) stated the relationship between urbanisation and GDP per capita, the result of study shows that GDP per capita is likely to not have relationship with mental health status in each country at 0.05 significant level. Due to the study from Chen et al. (2015) which indicated that there are more stable and well-planned in urban planning in developed countries, whereas the rapid growth of developing country could lead changes in cities characteristics and difficulty to adapt, we would summarise that even one country has high level of urbanisation, there is not necessary that country would face the living condition among population.

Future recommendation

It is necessary to balance both aspects – urbanisation and mental health condition across the world. Since the report of study shows that there is positive correlation between total urban population rate and mental health disorders prevalence, it could also summarise that the rising of urbanisation involves changes in social support and economic events which regarding to people’s health. In urban context, it is crucial to focus and raise awareness on mental health issues, not even focus on achieving the economic goal or raising the growth of cities.
